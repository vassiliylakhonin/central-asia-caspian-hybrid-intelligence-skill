# Central Asia / Caspian risk archetypes

A reference catalogue of risk archetypes that recur in Central Asia and Caspian analysis. Use them as **patterns** to structure reasoning, not as factual claims about any specific entity, route or jurisdiction. Operational use requires source-backed verification.

For each archetype: mechanism → typical indicators → evidence needed → common false positives → watch-next triggers → role-based mitigation questions.

---

## 1. Re-export / transshipment exposure

- **Mechanism**: goods enter a third country, are re-papered or re-routed, and reach a sanctioned end-user or jurisdiction.
- **Typical indicators**: HS-code import spikes inconsistent with domestic demand; mirror-data divergence with origin country; rapid rise in shell trading companies; route concentration on a small number of border crossings.
- **Evidence needed**: customs data on both legs, BO of trading entities, transport documents, end-user declarations.
- **False positives**: legitimate demand growth, regional warehousing, supply-chain diversification.
- **Watch-next triggers**: new high-priority items lists, trade-statistics anomalies, enforcement actions against forwarders.
- **Mitigation questions (role-based)**: bank — EDD on trading companies and BO; fintech — corridor- and HS-code-level monitoring; investor — concentration of revenue in suspect HS codes; logistics — end-user and routing documentation quality.

## 2. Dual-use goods routing

- **Mechanism**: items with civilian and military uses (electronics, optics, machinery, chemicals) are routed through Central Asia toward a restricted end-user.
- **Typical indicators**: imports of specific HS codes inconsistent with local industrial base; new entities concentrated in a narrow product set; export licences not aligned with declared end-use.
- **Evidence needed**: HS-code-level customs data, export-licence records where public, BO and end-user.
- **False positives**: genuine local manufacturing growth, regional distribution hubs.
- **Watch-next triggers**: regulator export-control updates, designations of named forwarders, joint-statement guidance from US/EU/UK.
- **Mitigation questions**: bank — sectoral red flags; fintech — merchant-category exclusions; logistics — refusal protocols and screening.

## 3. Correspondent banking de-risking

- **Mechanism**: tier-1 banks restrict or terminate respondent relationships in the region pre-emptively to manage their own enforcement risk.
- **Typical indicators**: rising rejection rates, longer settlement times, sudden requests for enhanced documentation, narrowing list of acceptable respondents.
- **Evidence needed**: respondent-bank communications, settlement and rejection metrics, public enforcement actions.
- **False positives**: routine periodic reviews, system migrations, individual-customer issues.
- **Watch-next triggers**: enforcement actions against regional respondents, new circumvention guidance, Western government joint communiqués.
- **Mitigation questions**: bank — diversification of correspondents; fintech — rail redundancy plan; investor — correspondent concentration risk.

## 4. Beneficial ownership opacity

- **Mechanism**: layered ownership structures, nominee directors and offshore vehicles obscure the natural person ultimately benefiting from a flow.
- **Typical indicators**: nominee directors, address sharing across many entities, missing or stale BO filings, frequent ownership changes.
- **Evidence needed**: company registry records, BO disclosures, court records, leaked or open corporate datasets handled per local law.
- **False positives**: legitimate trust structures, family holdings, regulated funds.
- **Watch-next triggers**: BO-register reforms, leaks affecting regional structures, regulator sanctions on opaque entities.
- **Mitigation questions**: bank — EDD escalation rules; fintech — onboarding thresholds; investor — diligence depth on layered targets.

## 5. Sanctioned-party adjacency

- **Mechanism**: counterparty is not designated but is owned, controlled by, or substantively linked to a designated party (50%-rule equivalents and control tests).
- **Typical indicators**: shared addresses, directors, phone numbers; immediate post-designation incorporation of successor entities.
- **Evidence needed**: registry data, transactional records, public statements, network-graph analysis.
- **False positives**: coincidental name overlap, common service-provider addresses.
- **Watch-next triggers**: regulator guidance on control and ownership tests, enforcement on adjacency cases.
- **Mitigation questions**: bank — control-test framework; fintech — successor-entity detection; investor — post-designation continuity risk.

## 6. Energy / logistics chokepoint risk

- **Mechanism**: a small number of pipelines, ports, rail links or border crossings carry a disproportionate share of flows; disruption cascades.
- **Typical indicators**: high concentration on a single node (e.g. a port, pipeline, border crossing); limited or undeveloped alternatives.
- **Evidence needed**: throughput data, pipeline/grid operator disclosures, capacity studies.
- **False positives**: short-term operational outages with quick recovery.
- **Watch-next triggers**: capacity announcements, incidents, geopolitical shocks affecting a node.
- **Mitigation questions**: operator — redundancy planning; investor — single-node revenue concentration; energy buyer — alternative-route economics.

## 7. Customs / statistics anomaly

- **Mechanism**: divergence between a country's reported imports/exports and trading-partner mirror data, or between sectoral statistics and production realities.
- **Typical indicators**: large mirror-data gaps; sudden re-classification of HS codes; statistical revisions clustered around sensitive items.
- **Evidence needed**: customs data on both sides, official statistics, partner disclosures.
- **False positives**: methodology changes, classification updates, free-zone reporting differences.
- **Watch-next triggers**: continued and widening mirror gaps; cross-border investigative reporting.
- **Mitigation questions**: analyst — adjust priors; bank — corridor-level monitoring; regulator — investigative referral.

## 8. State-capacity enforcement gap

- **Mechanism**: laws exist on paper (BO disclosure, AML, export control) but enforcement is uneven, under-resourced, or politically constrained.
- **Typical indicators**: low conviction or fine rates, public regulatory communications without follow-through, rapid roll-back of enforcement actions.
- **Evidence needed**: enforcement statistics, FATF reports, court records, regulator communications.
- **False positives**: short capacity ramp-up periods, transitional regimes.
- **Watch-next triggers**: FATF grey-listing, change in regulator leadership, public enforcement push.
- **Mitigation questions**: bank — local-rule reliance limits; investor — country-risk premia; operator — local-permit dependency.

## 9. China / Russia / EU leverage competition

- **Mechanism**: external powers compete via finance, infrastructure, regulation, technology and influence; the regional government's bargaining power and policy mix shifts.
- **Typical indicators**: project funding patterns, technology-stack choices, regulatory alignment moves, visible diplomatic shifts.
- **Evidence needed**: project documents, official communiqués, financing terms, customs and FDI flows.
- **False positives**: rhetorical alignment without operational follow-through.
- **Watch-next triggers**: financing terms on major projects, BRI/EU/US announcements, technology-stack decisions in critical sectors.
- **Mitigation questions**: investor — alignment risk in sector; operator — tech and standards exposure; bank — funding-source transparency.

## 10. Caspian corridor disruption

- **Mechanism**: Middle Corridor / TITR-type routes face capacity, weather, port, rail or political constraints; flows reroute and re-price.
- **Typical indicators**: rising dwell times at Caspian ports, ferry capacity constraints, rail bottlenecks, regulatory friction at borders.
- **Evidence needed**: port and rail operator data, freight rates, dwell-time stats.
- **False positives**: seasonal variability, single-event outages.
- **Watch-next triggers**: capacity expansions, multi-modal investments, geopolitical events on the route.
- **Mitigation questions**: shipper — alternative-route economics; investor — corridor concentration; energy/commodity trader — basis-risk on routing.

## 11. Regulatory arbitrage

- **Mechanism**: actors structure activity through the most permissive regime available across a multi-jurisdictional region.
- **Typical indicators**: clustering of entities in particular SEZs, free zones or jurisdictions; structural mismatch between activity and licensing.
- **Evidence needed**: licensing data, registry data, transaction records.
- **False positives**: legitimate jurisdictional choice based on tax or commercial rationale.
- **Watch-next triggers**: harmonization moves between regional regulators, enforcement on free-zone abuses.
- **Mitigation questions**: regulator — harmonization priorities; bank — jurisdiction-of-incorporation risk; investor — durability of regulatory edge.

## 12. Payment-rail exposure

- **Mechanism**: dependency on a specific payment rail (USD correspondent, SWIFT, card scheme, CNY rail, instant-payment system) creates concentrated risk if access changes.
- **Typical indicators**: single-rail dominance in revenue, lack of fallback, rail-specific terms changing.
- **Evidence needed**: rail-mix data, scheme communications, regulator decisions.
- **False positives**: short-term scheme outages.
- **Watch-next triggers**: scheme exits, regulator decisions on local rails, changes in card-scheme participation.
- **Mitigation questions**: fintech — rail diversification; bank — scheme-membership risk; merchant — acceptance-rail mix.

---

Use these archetypes as a *starting structure*. A real memo should select the relevant subset, explain the specific mechanism for the question at hand, and label evidence accordingly.
