## User question

> "We are an energy buyer / project investor with exposure to Caspian and Central Asian oil flows — Kazakhstan via CPC and BTC, Azerbaijan via BTC, with Caspian-corridor onward and northern (Druzhba) connections. Over the next 12 months, what corridor and route risks should we watch and how should we structure exposure?"

## Bottom line

The dominant near-term risk is **single-route concentration on the Russia-traversing Caspian Pipeline Consortium (CPC) for Kazakhstani crude**, layered with sanctions and counterparty-adjacency overlay on the financing, shipping and offtake chain. Around 80% of Kazakhstan's crude exports still leave through CPC to Novorossiysk in 2024 [S1, S2]. Diversification is real but small in absolute terms relative to baseline: BTC carried 1.4 mln tons of Kazakhstan oil in 2024 with a 2025 target of 1.7 mln tons against a long-term BTC capacity ambition of ~20 mln tons; Atyrau–Samara at 8.8 mln tons; Aktau seaport at 3.6 mln tons; Atasu–Alashankou (China) at 1.2 mln tons [S2]. EU re-pricing creates demand-side opportunity (EU crude from Russia fell from 27% in 2021 to 3% in 2024 per the same source) [S2]. Azerbaijan ships ~83% of its oil via BTC [S1]. `Judgment` informed by [S1, S2].

For an energy buyer / investor: **plan for slow diversification, not fast displacement of CPC**, and recognise that the binding constraints are physical (port, pipeline, ferry capacity) and structural (sanctions overlay on counterparties and shipping), not just commercial.

## Primary risk driver

`Primary driver is:` continued dominance of the Russia-routed CPC pipeline as the export channel for Kazakhstani crude (~80% in 2024) [S1, S2], combined with sanctions and political-economy leverage held by transit and financing counterparties along all Caspian-area export routes.

## Risk transmission mechanism

1. Kazakhstan produced 1.5 mln b/d crude in 2024 (EIA), with 1.9 mln b/d petroleum-and-other-liquids; total 2024 oil exports were 68.6 mln tons of which 54.9 mln tons (~80%) went via CPC to Novorossiysk in Russia [S1, S2]. CPC is therefore the binding export channel for the bulk of Kazakhstani volumes.
2. Diversification routes are real but small in absolute terms relative to CPC: BTC carried **1.4 mln tons of Kazakhstan oil in 2024** with a target of **1.7 mln tons in 2025** and Jan–Apr 2025 actuals of 489,000 tons; long-term ambition for BTC capacity is **20 mln tons** [S2]. Atyrau–Samara (to Russia and onward) is 8.8 mln tons; Aktau seaport 3.6 mln tons; Atasu–Alashankou (to China) 1.2 mln tons [S2].
3. Northern route (Druzhba to Germany) carried 1.5 mln tons in 2024, 1.1 mln tons in Jan–Jul 2025; Hungary received 85,000 tons in August 2025 via Croatia [S2]. These are politically and contractually sensitive routes.
4. Azerbaijan's BTC carries about **83% of Azerbaijani oil exports** [S1]; production is constrained — ~600,000 b/d projected average for 2024–2026 [S1].
5. Production-side bottlenecks identified by EIA include seasonal power outages affecting Kazakh oil production and insufficient natural-gas processing capacity at Karachaganak; Turkmenistan reported significant methane leakage from western fields (2.6 Mt methane in 2022) [S1].
6. Sanctions overlay: post-Ukraine, CPC is described as a "less favored trade route" since sanctions imposition; a Baku–Tikhoretsk pipeline reversal has been proposed to supply Azerbaijan independently of Russian infrastructure [S1]. Counterparty-adjacency risk on traders, shipowners, terminal operators and financiers along all routes remains in the regime.
7. Demand-side: EU crude imports from Russia fell from 27% in 2021 to 3% in 2024 [S2], creating an opening for Caspian alternatives — but absorbing this opening at scale requires the physical capacity (ports, ferry, pipeline, BTC throughput) that the World Bank's Middle Corridor study and the EBRD/EC sustainable transport study identify as gap-bound [S3, S4].
8. Methane and CBAM-style EU climate regulation overlay: long-term offtake economics for Caspian fossil flows depend on EU carbon-border-adjustment-style measures and methane-regulation enforcement; flagged here, not analysed in depth.

## Sanctions / compliance overlay

- Western sanctions and price-cap regimes affect specific origins, counterparties and shipping practices; secondary-sanctions guidance reaches counterparties along the corridor. Real status of any specific operator, trader, shipowner or financier requires direct screening against current OFAC SDN, BIS Entity List, EU consolidated, OFSI consolidated and UN lists at the point of use. `Unknown` here for any specific entity.
- CPC and Atyrau–Samara routes traverse Russia; counterparty and infrastructure-adjacency risk requires monitoring even where direct designations have not been made.
- Druzhba and Hungary/Croatia route receive specific EU-level political and contractual attention; verify legal status against the current EU consolidated sanctions list and Council legal acts (Regulation (EU) No 833/2014 amendments).

## Leverage shifts

- **Gains leverage**: BTC operators and Azerbaijan as transit; alternative ports (Aktau, Alat) as capacity ramps; Chinese offtake routes (Atasu–Alashankou) as diversification; financiers willing to underwrite sanctions-adjacent corridor risk.
- **Loses leverage**: producers structurally locked into CPC; financiers with concentrated CPC exposure; buyers without contractual flexibility on origin and route.

## Trigger points (watch-next)

- Material outages or capacity shifts on CPC (single-route 80% concentration risk) [S1, S2].
- BTC throughput updates against the 1.7 mln-ton 2025 target and 20-mln-ton long-term ambition [S2].
- Aktau / Alat / Caspian ferry capacity announcements [S3, S4].
- New OFAC / EU / UK designations affecting traders, shipowners or operators with corridor exposure.
- Price-cap and methane-regulation updates from G7 / EU.
- Karachaganak gas-processing capacity decisions [S1].
- Druzhba and Hungary/Croatia-route political / contractual events [S2].
- Major project-finance approvals or withdrawals from IFIs and major commercial banks.

## Role-based actions

- **Energy buyer**: stress-test single-route exposure for each contract; price-in basis-risk between CPC and alternatives; build optionality in tenor and volume; align sanctions-screening on shipowners and traders. *Subject to commercial and compliance review.*
- **Producer**: maintain redundancy in shipping and terminal access; negotiate transit-tariff stability; align production planning with capacity-ramp realism (BTC 1.4 → 1.7 Mt is *small*, not *fast*) [S2].
- **Project investor**: scrutinize single-route revenue concentration; assess financing-source durability under Western sanctions and ESG / methane-regulation overlays; price-in 12-month enforcement scenarios.
- **Financier / IFI**: structure tenor and covenants to absorb route-disruption scenarios; align KYC / sanctions screening across the financing chain.
- **Insurer**: corridor-specific war / political-risk pricing; align with route-specific incident history and shipping practices.
- **Operator (pipeline / port / terminal)**: communicate capacity and dwell-time transparently; coordinate with transit regulators on permitting and incident response.

All actions are illustrative. Real implementation requires qualified commercial, technical, compliance and legal counsel.

## Confidence

`Confidence`: medium for the structural mechanism (CPC concentration → leverage by transit/financing counterparties → re-pricing on stress; diversification real but absolutely small), grounded in retrieved primary EIA and authoritative regional reporting; low for any time-sensitive claim about today's specific operator or designation without re-verification.

## What would change the judgment

- A material increase in BTC throughput closer to the 20-mln-ton ambition that displaces a meaningful share of CPC volumes.
- A serious sustained outage at CPC, BTC or a Caspian port that re-prices the corridor for a multi-month period.
- A major sanctions or price-cap shift affecting routing or counterparty options.
- Methane-regulation or CBAM-style measure that durably re-prices Caspian fossil offtake.
- A targeted designation that names specific operators, traders or financiers with corridor exposure (would sharply raise risk, not lower it).
