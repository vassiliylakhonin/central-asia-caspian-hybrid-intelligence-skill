## Bottom line

`[analyst-judgment]` For a European trade-finance bank, the binding 2026–2027 exposure on the Russia–Iran–China junction is **not** any single designation event. It is the **distribution of secondary-sanctions risk, correspondent-rail loss, and reputational concentration** across a small number of recurring structural patterns — Caspian transit ambiguity, Iran-conduit settlement, and Chinese intermediation opacity — that compound each other when they appear in the same transaction. A limits framework built around individual list checks under-prices the joint distribution. A limits framework built around the **junction patterns** prices it correctly.

The bank's structural exposure transmits through six channels: (1) secondary-sanctions risk under US/EU/UK regimes; (2) correspondent-bank de-risking and rail loss; (3) export-control exposure on dual-use goods; (4) ownership opacity in counterparty chains; (5) settlement currency and rail substitution risk; (6) reputational concentration across the bank's wider book. The right response is a **junction-aware tiered framework** with explicit Stop-and-request triggers, not a per-name screening upgrade.

## Primary driver

`Primary driver [analyst-judgment]:` The post-2022 sanctions environment has structurally re-routed a non-trivial share of Russia-bound and Russia-origin commercial flow through three corridors: (a) the Caspian / Middle Corridor land bridge through Central Asia; (b) Iran as a settlement and transit conduit; (c) Chinese intermediaries acting as paying agent, supplier of record, or commodity intermediary. Each corridor in isolation has known risk; the **junction** — where two or three appear in the same transaction — is what trade-finance banks under-monitor.

The driver is not "sanctions enforcement is intensifying" (an over-generic claim); it is **flow concentration into a small set of recurring junction patterns** that share counterparty types, document types, and settlement structures. The patterns repeat across sectors (electronics, machinery, dual-use components, oil products, agricultural inputs, metals).

## Why now

`[analyst-judgment]` Three structural reasons make 2026–2027 the relevant horizon:

1. The post-2022 routing patterns have had time to stabilize into recognizable typologies; pre-2024 banks were responding to incidents, post-2025 banks should be responding to typologies.
2. Secondary-sanctions doctrine under US E.O.s and EU/UK regimes has clarified that non-US/EU/UK banks transacting with sanctioned-nexus counterparties carry exposure regardless of jurisdiction — the **conduit-by-conduit** posture has hardened `[secondary][verify]`.
3. Correspondent-bank de-risking has accelerated in jurisdictions where junction-pattern exposure is documented; the **rail-loss risk** has moved from tail-risk to baseline.

## Mechanism

### The three patterns

1. **Caspian transit ambiguity.** A counterparty in a Central Asian jurisdiction (Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, Azerbaijan, Turkmenistan, Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan) ships goods nominally for in-region consumption but the goods structurally re-export to Russia, Iran, or via Caspian/Middle Corridor to onward destinations. Customs mirror-statistic divergence is one diagnostic; see [`live-source-backed-customs-statistics-anomaly.md`](live-source-backed-customs-statistics-anomaly.md) for the source-anchored case.
2. **Iran-conduit settlement.** A non-Iran counterparty settles transactions through an Iran-connected payment or correspondent path — directly (Iran-domiciled bank or commercial entity acting as payment agent) or indirectly (Iran-linked intermediary in a third jurisdiction). The conduit may be a state-aligned, IRGC-affiliated, or Iran-private commercial entity; the AML and sanctions exposure differs across these three. Apply the Gulf + Middle East skill's Iran actor distinction taxonomy.
3. **Chinese intermediation opacity.** A Chinese counterparty appears as buyer of record, paying agent, or supplier, with limited UBO transparency on the actual onward flow. Chinese intermediation is not categorically sanctions-exposed; opacity is the binding variable, not nationality `[analyst-judgment]`.

`[inference]` The junction risk is the **product**, not the sum, of these patterns. A transaction with one pattern is a known-elevated risk. A transaction with two or three is a different risk category — it should not be priced by adding the individual scores.

### Transmission to bank exposure

For a European trade-finance bank, exposure transmits through:

- **Secondary-sanctions exposure under US E.O.s and EU/UK regimes** `[secondary][verify]`. Conduct that facilitates a sanctioned-nexus transaction can attract sanctions adjacency for the financing bank, even when the bank is non-US and the counterparty is non-sanctioned at the entity level.
- **Correspondent-bank rail loss.** Counterparty banks (especially USD-clearing and EUR-clearing relationships) tier their respondent-bank exposure by junction pattern; a respondent bank with junction-pattern flow concentration faces rail-loss risk under standard tiering.
- **Export-control exposure** on dual-use, advanced electronics, machinery components — distinct from financial sanctions and screened against different regimes.
- **AML transmission** where junction patterns increase the probability of beneficial-ownership opacity, layered settlement, or structured-volume patterns.
- **Reputational concentration** across the trade book — a single named enforcement action in a junction pattern propagates across the wider portfolio's headline risk.
- **Settlement-currency risk** — yuan, rouble, or other non-G7 settlement reduces sanctions-rail exposure but does not eliminate secondary-sanctions exposure, and shifts the risk from financial-rail to enforcement-narrative `[analyst-judgment]`.

## Exposure map

| Exposure concentration | Type | Control point |
|---|---|---|
| Junction-pattern flow concentration not visible in name-based screening | Sanctions / secondary-sanctions risk | Pattern-aware EDD trigger framework (independent of named-entity screening) |
| Loss of USD- and EUR-clearing rails after correspondent tiering | Operational / commercial risk | Diversified clearing relationships; defensive disclosure to correspondents on pattern controls |
| Dual-use export-control exposure on machinery / electronics / advanced inputs | Export-control regime | HS-code-based pre-clearance discipline; supplier UBO + end-use chain documentation |
| Counterparty UBO opacity in Chinese-intermediation legs | AML / financial-crime risk | UBO disclosure thresholds; refuse-or-Stop-and-request policy on opacity beyond threshold |
| Iran-conduit detection in settlement chain | Sanctions / OFAC SDN risk | Conduit-mapping in payment instructions; refuse on Iran-state and IRGC-affiliated nexus; case-by-case on Iran-private commercial |
| Reputation cascade from a single enforcement action | Reputation / governance risk | Board-level limits on junction-pattern concentration; explicit disclosure to risk committee |

`[analyst-judgment]` Concentrated exposures for a typical European trade-finance bank: (1) limits frameworks priced on named-entity screening rather than pattern density; (2) under-investment in pattern-detection EDD relative to investment in list-screening tooling; (3) reputational concentration when 3–5 named transactions across multiple sectors share one junction pattern.

## Role-based implications

**Head of sanctions / financial crime:**
- Move limits framework from named-entity tiering to **junction-pattern tiering**. Build a 4-pattern matrix (single / double / triple-junction; pattern type) with limits, EDD, and Stop-and-request triggers per cell.
- Establish a pattern-density metric independent of named-entity screening; report it to the board alongside designation-screening metrics.
- Pre-agree refuse-or-Stop-and-request triggers with first-line so escalation is not ad-hoc when a multi-pattern transaction arrives.

**Head of trade / supply-chain finance:**
- Re-price junction-pattern concentration into ticket pricing — not only as a risk control but as a margin question.
- Engage first-line on training: the junction patterns are recognisable in document flow if first-line knows what to look for.
- Maintain a stop-the-line authority for first-line on triple-junction transactions independent of name screen.

**Correspondent-bank relationship management:**
- Build a defensive disclosure pack on the bank's junction-pattern controls — proactively shareable with USD- and EUR-clearing correspondents. The risk is **silent tiering**: rail status changes without prior signal.
- Treat correspondent rail loss as a P&L-line risk, not only an ops risk; price the optionality of rail diversification.

**Board risk committee:**
- Add **junction-pattern concentration** to the standing risk dashboard. A bank can be designation-clean and still rail-exposed and reputation-exposed on pattern grounds.
- Set an explicit appetite ceiling on triple-junction transactions (not zero; explicit cap, with named approval below).
